Information About Common Mental Illnesses

Educating yourself about the types, causes, symptoms, and treatments of mental illness is the first step to understanding yourself and your loved ones and to erasing the stigma surrounding these very common conditions

Click on any title below to learn more.


Learn About: Depression

What is depression?

Depression is one of the most common mental disorders in the world. Approximately 350 million people suffer from depression worldwide and millions more are affected by their loved ones' struggles. Depression takes several forms, including major depression, persistent depressive disorder, and bipolar disorder.


What are the symptoms?

The symptoms of depression are varied and can affect all aspects of a person's life. Some examples include feelings of deep sadness, moodiness, hopelessness and loss of interest in activities. Other symptoms are exhaustion, changes in appetite, and abnormal thoughts like self-harm or trouble concentrating.


What causes depression?

The symptoms of depression are caused by changes in the brain's chemistry. These can be brought on by genetics, hormone fluctuation, some medical conditions, excessive stress, grief or difficult living circumstances.


Types of Depression
  • Persistent Depressive Disorder

    • A persistent and depressive mood that lasts at least two years. Some periods of less severe depressive mood accompanied by intense bouts of depression for 2 or more years is considered persistent depression.

  • Perinatal Depression

    • Extreme feelings of sadness, exhaustion, and anxiety following a birth or pregnancy that hinders a mother’s ability to care for her child and accomplish daily activities.

  • Psychotic Depression

    • Psychotic depression applies to individuals who experience both severe depression and strong beliefs in false pretenses such as delusions and hallucinations. These hallucinations mainly center around depressive thoughts/emotions such as guilt, poverty, or illness.

  • Seasonal Affective Disorder

    • Usually, Seasonal Affective Depression manifests during the winter season and lifts around spring and summer. This form of depression revolves around feelings of exhaustion (increased amount of sleep), social withdrawal, and weight gain. This depression will usually appear consistently every year around winter.

Learn About: Anxiety

Anxiety is a common and inescapable part of life. However, for most it is temporary. This is not the case for those with anxiety disorders. People who suffer from an anxiety disorder can feel anxiety nearly constantly, and have physical symptoms which interfere with their daily life.

This may sound extreme. However, it is not rare. In the United States, the most common type of mental disorder is an anxiety disorder. 18% of American adults experience some type of anxiety disorder.


Types of Anxiety

Like depression, there are many types of anxiety disorders. Some examples are General Anxiety Disorder (GAD), Panic Disorder, or Social Anxiety. Obsessive-Compulsive Disorder (OCD) and Post-Traumatic Stress Disorder (PTSD) are also considered anxiety disorders.

  • General Anxiety Disorder

    • Those who suffer from GAD experience persistent tension and worry over everyday situations or over nothing in particular. For them, the typical anxiety of life is multiplied and constant.

  • Panic Disorder

    • Those with Panic Disorder experience panic attacks. Panic attacks, also called anxiety attacks, are intense periods in which a person may experience feelings of fear and doom, chest pain, dizziness, shortness of breath, and sweating.

  • Social Anxiety

    • Social anxiety causes overwhelming worry about typical social situations. Those with social anxiety often fear being judged or acting ‘wrong’. The fear can be so bad that they avoid social situations all together.

  • Obsessive-Compulsive Disorder

    • OCD is a chronic condition in which a person has recurring thoughts (called obsessions) and behaviors (called compulsions) that they feel they must carry out over and over. The symptoms of OCD can interfere with work, school, and relationships. Those with OCD cannot control the thoughts or behaviors that OCD causes, and must carry them out to relieve the anxiety the thoughts cause.

  • Post-Traumatic Stress Disorder

    • PTSD can develop in a person after they have experienced a traumatic event. It causes types of symptoms like re-experiencing, avoidance, reactivity, and cognition. People with PTSD may experience flashbacks and nightmares, stay away from places and things that remind them of the event, be ‘on-edge’ or easily angered, and experience cognitive changes like loss of memory or guilt. PTSD is common amongst veterans or young children who have experienced trauma.



Learn About: Bipolar Disorder

What is bipolar disorder?

Bipolar disorder is grouped as a subset of depression, characterized by extreme mood swings between periods of exhaustion and depression and those of high-energy euphoria (mania). The mood shifts can occur over months or during the course of a week.


What do the shifts involve?

During the mania phase, a person can feel euphoria and high energy. However, this often goes too far and leads to irritability and dangerous, reckless behavior. A person with bipolar disorder can also experience hypomania, a mild type of mania that doesn’t involve psychosis.

After mania comes a depressed phase, in which a person can have depressive symptoms so bad that they cannot leave bed.


Treatment and Types

There are several types of bipolar disorders: bipolar I, bipolar II, and cyclothymic disorder. Each one can be treated but treatment must be ongoing for life. Bipolar disorder is a chronic illness and will only worsen with time if untreated.



Learn About: Stress

What is stress?

Stress is usually described as an overwhelming feeling produced in response to stressors (Events, objects, people that cause you stress) that invoke the fight or flight response; resulting in the release of adrenaline and cortisol. Throughout life, we continually analyze particular situations, people, and events to determine if our skill set and mental capacity are capable of confronting this situation. When we are stressed, we have carefully analyzed a particular situation and deemed ourselves lacking the tools to tackle this challenge and thus feel some sort of stress and anxiety over the situation. Logically speaking, stress is truly imperative to life; originating in our primitive instincts to fight or escape in reaction to some imminent threat, and motivating us to survive some situation our brain interpret as a threat. So while low-levels of stress can be viewed as helpful in boosting brainpower and focus, too much of anything can be harmful, and stress is no exception. High levels of stress lead to heart disease, a weaker immune system, and increase aging.
